# Vocabulary Terms
___________
1. **Political Culture**: The widely shared beliefs, values, and norms concerning the relationship of citizens to government and to one another within a society.
    
2. **Political Socialization**: The process by which individuals acquire their political beliefs, values, and orientations, often from family, schools, media, and peers.
    
3. **Globalization**: The increasing interconnectedness and interdependence of nations through trade, communication, and cultural exchange, impacting domestic politics and economies.
    
4. **Public Opinion Polls**: Surveys used to measure the attitudes, beliefs, or preferences of a population regarding political issues or candidates.
    
5. **Straw Polls**: Informal, non-scientific surveys of opinion, often used to gauge initial reactions rather than to predict outcomes accurately.
    
6. **Push Polling**: A deceptive campaign technique disguised as a poll, which presents negative or false information about an opponent to "push" voters away from them.
    
7. **Sampling (Random and Representative Samples)**: The method of selecting a subset of individuals from a population to estimate characteristics of the whole group. A **random sample** gives every member an equal chance of selection. A **representative sample** mirrors the demographics of the larger population.
    
8. **Margin of Error**: A statistical measure expressing the amount of random sampling error in a poll's results, indicating the range within which the true population value likely lies.
    
9. **Focus Group**: A small, diverse group of people assembled to discuss and provide detailed feedback on a candidate, product, or issue, guided by a moderator.
    
10. **Approval Ratings**: A percentage indicating the proportion of the population that approves of the job performance of a public official, typically the president.
    
11. **Free Enterprise System**: An economic system characterized by private ownership of businesses, market competition, and minimal government intervention.
    
12. **Individualism**: A belief in the primary importance of the individual and in the virtues of self-reliance and personal independence, central to American political culture.
    
13. **Mandatory vs. Discretionary Spending**: **Mandatory spending** is government spending required by law on entitlement programs like Social Security. **Discretionary spending** is government spending implemented through an appropriations bill for programs like defense or education.
    
14. **Medicaid vs Medicare**: **Medicaid** is a joint federal and state program that provides health insurance for low-income individuals. **Medicare** is a federal program that provides health insurance for people aged 65 and older and some younger people with disabilities.
    
15. **Social Welfare**: Government programs designed to provide financial aid, services, or support to individuals in need, such as the poor, disabled, or elderly.
    
16. **Suffrage**: The right to vote in political elections.
    
17. **Primary Elections (Direct, Blanket, Runoff, Open and Closed)**: Elections where voters select a party's nominee for the general election. Types include: **Direct Primary** (voters choose nominees directly), **Blanket Primary** (voters can choose candidates from any party for each office), **Runoff Primary** (a second primary between the top two candidates if no one wins a majority), **Open Primary** (voters may choose in which party's primary to vote), and **Closed Primary** (only registered party members may vote).
    
18. **Recall**: A procedure that allows citizens to remove and replace an elected official before the end of their term, usually through a petition and a special election.
    
19. **Referendum**: A direct vote by the electorate on a proposed law, constitutional amendment, or public policy that has been passed by the legislature.
    
20. **Initiative (voting)**: A process that allows citizens to propose a new law or constitutional amendment and place it on the ballot for voter approval, usually by gathering signatures on a petition.
    
21. **Political Efficacy**: An individual's belief in their ability to understand and influence political events. **Internal efficacy** is belief in one's own competence. **External efficacy** is belief that the government will respond to citizens.
    
22. **Life Cycle Effect**: The theory that people's political attitudes and participation change as they age, such as becoming more conservative or more likely to vote.
    
23. **Voting Blocs**: Groups of voters who are strongly motivated by a specific common concern or demographic characteristic (e.g., religion, ethnicity, union membership) to vote together.
    
24. **Caucus**: A local meeting of party members to select delegates to a state or national convention, or to express preferences for presidential candidates.
    
25. **Coattail Effect**: The influence a popular or unpopular candidate at the top of the ticket (e.g., for president) can have on the election of other candidates from the same party further down the ballot.
    
26. **Realigning Elections**: A major, long-term shift in the fundamental political allegiance and coalition of groups within the electorate, often following a critical election.
    
27. **Dealigning Elections**: A period where party loyalty weakens and voters become more independent, leading to less predictable election outcomes and split-ticket voting.
    
28. **Split-Ticket Voting vs Straight Ticket Voting**: **Split-ticket voting** is voting for candidates from different political parties for different offices in the same election. **Straight-ticket voting** is voting for all candidates from a single political party.
    
29. **Candidate Centered Campaign**: A campaign strategy that focuses on the personal qualities and agenda of a specific candidate rather than on broad party platforms or ideology.
    
30. **Poll Taxes**: A fee required to vote, historically used in some U.S. states to disenfranchise poor and minority voters; prohibited by the 24th Amendment.
    
31. **White Primaries**: Primary elections held in some Southern states from the 1890s to the 1940s where only white voters were permitted to participate, effectively excluding Black voters; ruled unconstitutional.
    
32. **Voter Apathy**: A lack of interest, motivation, or concern about voting and the electoral process among eligible voters.
